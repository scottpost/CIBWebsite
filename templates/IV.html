Math Introduction

Historical Volatility

Historical volatility refers to the standard deviation (square root of variance) of daily returns over a time period of n days.

$$HV_{\text{daily}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n - 1} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu_x)^2}$$

$$x_i\text{: return on day i}$$

$$\mu_x\text{: mean return}$$

Option Pricing and Implied Volatility

Option pricing can be estimated using the Black-Scholes-Merton model:

$$\begin{align*} C(S_t, t) &= N(d_1)S_t - N(d_2)Ke^{-r(T-t)}\\ d_1 &= \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{T-t}} [ln(\frac{S_t}{K}) + (r + \frac{\sigma^2}{2})(T-t)]\\ d_2 &= d_1 - \sigma \sqrt{T-t}\\ \end{align*}$$

All of these variables are assumed to be known, except for σ (implied volatility), making it crucial for pricing of options.

Volatility Mechanics

Implied volatility is the combination of the expected variance of the underlying asset (over the lifetime of the option) and the volatility risk premium as determined by the seller.

$$ \sigma_{iv} = E[\sigma_{realized}] + \sigma_{premium}$$


\(E[\sigma_{realized}]\) is the expected value of the volatility during the time period of the option. With future information, we can model it as the realized volatility (\(\sigma_{rv}\)) over the time period of the option. $$\sigma_{iv} = \sigma_{rv} + \sigma_{premium}$$


\(~68 \% \) of returns should fall within \(\pm \sigma_{rv}\). The \(\sigma_{premium}\) is included to make the buyer pay for a distribution with larger variance than reality, as a means of ensuring profit for the seller. Accordingly, \(\sigma_{iv}\) is always noticeably larger then \(\sigma_{rv}\).
